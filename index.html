Do Women (still) have to be naked to get into the Met ?

An Analysis of the Modern Art permanent Collection of the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York City

By Jaya Mirani

In the spring of 1989, a group of feminist artists known as "The Guerrilla Girls" paid the biggest museum in the USA, the Metropolitan Museum of Art, a visit. Surveying the works on display in the museum's Modern Art galleries, they discovered that not even 5 percent of the artists represented were women, while 85 percent of the nudes depicted female bodies. To call out this discrepancy, the group created their most famous work, an iconic poster of a naked female body wearing a gorilla mask, the trademark disguise of the Guerrilla Girls.

Established in the 80s, the Guerrilla Girls view themselves as the "conscience of the art world," denouncing the underrepresentation of female artists and artists of color. This year marks the 35th anniversary of their visit to the Met. So while I was walking around the Modern Art Wing of the Met, I asked myself: Do women still have to be naked to get into the Met?

The Met Museum has an extensive online database featuring their entire collection. Comparing the number of female vs. male artists * in the current and past Modern Art Collection shows that the change in the past three decades has been stunningly minimal.

* The data does not account for non binary or gender nonconforming artists, which is why I am just using male/female in this analysis.

In 1989 only about 20% of all artists in the Modern Art Collection identified as female (counting all works of the permanent collection whether on display or not). In the past 35 years this number has gone up only slightly, to 23%.

Artworks in the collection

The permanent Collection of the Met Museums Modern Art Department currently houses around 15.000 artworks - I used around 13.700 in the dataset for the project. Of these artworks, only 2197 were created by women.

Tracking Art Acquisitions at the Met

The Met has seemingly not done a lot in terms of aqcuisitions to change this underrepresentation. Tracking the aqcuisition of artworks from the time of the Museums establishment in the 1870s until today, shows that artworks by men were consistently bought more than artworks by women.

Top 5 female artists

But who are the female artists featured in the Metropolitan Museum of Art's permanent collection?

These are the five female artists with the most artworks in the Museum.

Dorothy Wright-Liebes (1897–1972)
American textile designer and weaver
Artworks in the Met: 89

Anni Albers (1899-1994)
German textile artist and weaver, teacher at the Bauhaus
Artworks in the Met: 80

Eva Zeisel (1906-2011)
Hungarian-born American industrial designer
Artworks in the Met: 66

Margarete Willers (1883–1977)
German weaver and painter, part of the Bauhaus
Artworks in the Met: 65

Vuokko Nurmesniemi (1930)
Finnish textile designer
Artworks in the Met: 52

All of these artists are designers, either of textiles or ceramics. What they have in common as well is that all of them are either from the USA or Europe. This is true for most of the female artists in the museum's permanent collection, showing that not only are female perspectives underrepresented but perspectives from the global South as well.

To return to the question from the beginning—whether women have to be naked to get into the Met. During my visits to the Modern and Contemporary Art galleries, I did not see a lot of artworks depicting naked female bodies by male artists. But I also did not see many artworks by female artists. And the data shows that the Guerrilla Girls might have to make their way back into the Metropolitan Museum of Art 35 years after their first visit.
